Long Journey from Home

At the age of nine, accompanying his exiled Father and family in their banishment, by order of the Shah of Persia, in the dead of an icy 1853 winter, with insufficient food and provisions, Abbás Effendi started his first journey by foot and horse to the Ottoman city of Baghdad where His family resided for nine years.  Later by an order of the Ottoman authorities, the family was further banished in 1863 to Constantinople, and then a few months later to Adrianople.  And finally, in 1868 traveling by small boats, unfit for a Mediterranean Sea voyage in the heat of summer, they were deposited into prison cells in the filthy, polluted ancient city of Akko, Palestine.   The expectation of the temporal and clerical powers was that the groundbreaking Teachings of Bahá’u'lláh to re-order individual and social relationships would be forever obliterated and forgotten.

Throughout Abbás Effendi’s lifetime of banishment there were no opportunities for formal education.   His primary moral and spiritual educator was his father.  In time, Abbas Effendi was known as 'Abdu'l-Bahá – “Servant of the Glory of God” – his preferred name. 'Abdu'l-Bahá became regarded as an outstanding member of the Bahá’í exile community.  As a teenager he was his father’s scribe and was regularly seen debating theological issues with the learned men of the area.

In 1908 when then those then in political and ecclesiastical power were ousted, 'Abdu'l-Bahá, the one appointed by Bahá’u'lláh to maintain the purity of His teachings after His passing in 1892, was released from imprisonment. By this time, 'Abdu'l-Bahá’s health had been weakened from several maladies resulting from the stresses and strains of a life spent almost completely in exile and imprisonment.  

However, in 1911, `Abdu'l-Bahá made the decision to journey to the West and present, to high and low alike, Bahá'u'lláh's prescription for the moral and spiritual renewal of society.  At the age of 67, 'Abdu'l-Bahá traveled by ships, trains and cars to the centers of the western world in European cities including Paris, London, Edinburgh, Stuttgart, Budapest, and Vienna, and also to North American cities including Montreal, Washington, Chicago, Cleveland, Pittsburgh, Montclair, Boston, Worcester, Brooklyn, Fanwood, Milford, Philadelphia, West Englewood, Jersey City, Cambridge, Medford, Morristown, Dublin, Green Acre, Montreal, Malden, Buffalo, Kenosha, Minneapolis, St. Paul, Omaha, Lincoln, Denver, Glenwood Springs, Salt Lake City, San Francisco, Oakland, Palo Alto, Berkeley, Pasadena, Los Angeles, Sacramento, Cincinnati, and Baltimore.

How did someone without formal education, being exiled and imprisoned throughout most of his life, succeed in delivering a message of peace and unity with brilliant simplicity, far from his remote Middle Eastern homeland to the Western shores of the United States?  How was it that His advice was sought after across America in peace societies and pulpits, by poor people, politicians, scholars and philosophers including such notable figures as Phoebe Hearst, Alexander Graham Bell, Andrew Carnegie and Dr. David S. Jordan, President of Stanford University - people of prominence in their fields of endeavor, offering insights into expanded realms of knowledge to all?  What impact did this journey have on those living throughout the world today?

The furthermost western portion of his journey to California might be considered as perhaps the most significant portion of His entire western journey.   Had it not been for this portion of His journey, His footsteps would not have trodden the entire North American continent, from the Atlantic to the Pacific coast.  Based on this continental experience, He later penned soul-inspiring messages to loved ones living in Canada, and the northern, southern, central and western portions of the United States.  These messages later became the basis of the development of a truly universal community of individuals whose efforts are guided by divinely conceived and inspired institutions striving a century later to guide others in ways to achieve human happiness at individual and societal levels worldwide.

Let us now join 'Abdu'l-Bahá in His twenty three day visit to California, viewing it from the diverse perspectives of his devoted followers, fascinated newspaper reporters, and admiring leaders of public thought.
